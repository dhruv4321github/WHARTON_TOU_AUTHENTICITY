# Part 4 — Is "rising authenticity" real, or just herding? (Plain-English Summary)

*An exploratory follow-on. The question, the test, and what it means for trusting
Part 3. Every number here is regenerated by `convergence.py` from the committed
Part 1/2/3 data.*

## The question
Part 3 found good news: across our 50 firms, what companies **say** they value and what
their disclosures **emphasize** moved *closer together* from 2016 to 2024. The tempting
story is "firms are increasingly walking their talk." Before believing that, I asked the
skeptical question: **what if firms didn't individually get more authentic — what if they
all just drifted onto the same fashionable corporate script?** If every company starts
using the same stakeholder/ESG/human-capital language in both its website and its
filings, "say" and "do" will line up automatically, with no real change in authenticity.
This is a classic pattern in organizational research (institutional "herding").

## The test
If firms are herding, they should look **more and more alike** over time. So I measured,
each year, how *spread out* the 50 firms are from the average company — once for their
website language, once for their disclosure language. Shrinking spread = convergence =
herding.

## What I found

**1. Firms' disclosures really did converge — strongly.** The spread between companies'
proxy-statement profiles fell steadily and significantly over 2016–2024. Their website
profiles also drifted together, but more weakly (and the website data is thinner, so I
treat that half as suggestive). **Herding is real, and it's concentrated in mandatory
disclosure.**

**2. So part of Part 3's good news *is* a mirage — but the part that matters survives.**
Part 3 reported two versions of alignment:
- a **raw** version, which I'd flagged as contaminated by "every filing sounds like a
  filing." It rose a lot — and we can now see *why*: as firms converge on a shared
  template, raw alignment climbs for everyone automatically. **That rise is largely
  herding, not authenticity.**
- a **peer-relative** version — Part 3's headline measure — which was *specifically
  designed* to strip out that shared-template effect by comparing each firm only to how
  it stands out from its peers. **It also rose.** Because this measure removes the common
  drift, its rise is *not* explained away by herding. **This is reassuring: it shows
  Part 3's central methodological choice was the right one — it's exactly the correction
  that separates genuine alignment from everyone-copying-everyone, and real alignment
  still showed up after the correction.**

The one honest catch: as firms converge, the distinctive "peer-relative" signal that the
headline measure relies on gets fainter, so the most recent years' scores rest on thinner
evidence. Trust the *direction* (alignment genuinely rose); be a little more cautious
about the exact late-year *levels*.

**3. The most interesting twist: sustainability is where firms *split apart*, not
together.** You'd expect the "values" themes — sustainability, diversity — to be where
everyone converged on the same green/inclusive language. The data show the opposite. The
convergence is concentrated in **money (financial/shareholder) and workforce
(human-capital) language** — the latter lining up almost exactly with a **2020 SEC rule**
that forced essentially every company to start disclosing human-capital information (a
regulation, not a fad). Meanwhile, companies became *more different from each other* on
**sustainability** (the spread between firms grew ~60% in disclosures). In other words,
during the very years of the "ESG backlash," climate/sustainability language became a
place where firms stake out **distinct positions** — some leaning in, some pulling back —
rather than a script everyone recites. The fault line in corporate self-presentation
isn't profits or people; it's the environment.

## Bottom line for a decision-maker
1. **Don't over-read rising "authenticity" numbers in aggregate** — when a whole industry
   adopts the same language, simple say-vs-do agreement goes up on its own. Always use the
   *peer-relative* version, which is built to ignore that.
2. **Part 3's peer-relative index passed its hardest stress test** — its upward trend is
   not just herding — so it's safe to use for the intended job (flagging individual firms
   whose talk and disclosure diverge), with a little extra caution on the latest years.
3. **Watch sustainability specifically.** It's the one theme where firms are actively
   *differentiating*, which makes it the most informative place to look for genuine
   value-positioning — and the most likely place for authenticity gaps to open up.
