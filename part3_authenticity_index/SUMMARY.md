# Part 3 — Organizational Authenticity Index (Plain-English Summary)

*This is for a reader who wants the idea, the findings, and whether to trust them.
The method and full column definitions are in this folder's README; every number
here is regenerated by `build_index.py` + `report_index.py` from the committed
Part 1 and Part 2 data.*

## The idea in one paragraph
Part 1 measured what each firm **says** it values (the themes on its "About/values"
web page). Part 2 measured what each firm's mandatory disclosures **suggest** it
prioritizes (the themes in its annual proxy statement). Both were scored on the
**same ten value themes**, so we can ask, firm by firm and year by year: *do the
things a company talks up match the things it actually emphasizes in disclosure?*
That gap — or alignment — is our **authenticity index**.

## How we define "alignment" (the key choice)
We compare each firm's **profile of emphasis**, not raw amounts (a glossy website
and a legal proxy use very different language, so only the *mix* of themes is
comparable). Crucially, we measure each firm **relative to its peers in the same
year**: the proxy of *every* company is heavy on finance and governance, so what
matters is whether the themes a firm stresses *more than other firms* in its words
are also the ones it stresses *more than other firms* in its disclosures. A score
near **+1** means a firm's distinctive talk and its distinctive disclosure point the
same way (authentic positioning); near **0** means no relationship; **negative**
means it talks up exactly the themes it plays down in disclosure. We also report a
reader-friendly **0–100 authenticity score** (50 = neutral). The measure covers
**375 company-years across 48 firms** (two firms, Apple and Berkshire Hathaway, had
web pages too sparse to score — an honest gap, not a zero).

## What we found

**1. There is real, wide variation — this isn't all noise.** Scores span the full
range from −0.79 to +0.80, and **44%** of company-years show a *say–do inversion*
(a firm distinctively talks up themes it distinctively de-emphasizes in disclosure).
Importantly, the differences are mostly **between firms, not year-to-year wobble**
(about 60% of the variation is cross-firm), so a firm's score is a reasonably
stable trait you can act on.

**2. Technology firms align best; Healthcare aligns worst.** Average alignment by
sector: **Technology (+0.17) > Consumer Discretionary (+0.12) ≈ Energy (+0.11) >
Financials (≈0) > Healthcare (−0.03)**. Tech firms' websites and proxies emphasize
a similar distinctive mix; Healthcare shows the widest gap between pitch and
disclosure.

**3. Say and do have converged over time.** Average alignment rose steadily from
**−0.02 in 2016 to +0.20 in 2024**. Over the window, the themes companies advertise
and the themes they disclose have moved *closer together* — consistent with both
their websites and their proxies adopting the same stakeholder / sustainability /
human-capital vocabulary, especially after 2020.

**4. The firm rankings pass a smell test.** The most aligned firms are **Lowe's,
TJX, Occidental, American Express, and IBM**; the least aligned are **UnitedHealth,
Abbott, BlackRock, Citigroup, and ConocoPhillips**. The lowest scorer,
**UnitedHealth**, presents itself around *innovation and technology* while its proxy
is distinctively tilted toward *financial/shareholder* themes versus peers — a
say–do gap that matches this company's well-aired public criticism. That the index
independently flags it is a good sign the measure is capturing something real.

## Why you can trust it (and where to be careful)
We ran three checks: (a) the top/bottom firms make intuitive sense (above);
(b) scores are stable within a firm over years, so they reflect the firm, not a
fluke year; and (c) our peer-relative adjustment genuinely changes the ranking
versus a naive comparison (rank correlation only ~0.36), confirming it strips out
the "all proxies sound like proxies" effect that would otherwise dominate.

**Three honest caveats.** (1) The index is **relative and rhetorical** — it compares
the *mix* of themes a firm emphasizes in words vs. disclosures; it does *not* verify
the firm's real-world behavior, and a high score means "talk and disclosure agree,"
not "good company." (2) It rests on **two automated text measurements** (an LLM
reading of web pages and a dictionary scan of proxies), each of which can mis-score
a theme. (3) Where a firm's web page wasn't archived in a given year we carry its
most recent prior page forward (flagged in the data); if the site changed silently,
that profile is stale. All three are documented in the README.

## Bottom line for a decision-maker
The index turns "are they walking the talk?" into a comparable, reproducible number
per firm per year. Use it to **flag firms whose pitch and disclosure diverge** (low
or negative scores — e.g. UnitedHealth) for a closer look, to **track whether a firm
is closing its say–do gap over time**, and to **benchmark within a sector**. Treat a
low score as a *prompt to investigate*, not a verdict — it points to where stated
values and disclosed priorities don't line up, which is exactly where authenticity
questions live.
